## Business Overview

### Mission
Using the bounty for good - preserving heritage produce and donating 100% of profits to support the community.

### Core Assets
- **2 Heritage Apple Trees** in Portland, OR
- Annual yield: ~1,000 lbs of apples
- Home garden producing diverse vegetables, herbs, and berries
- Dehydrator and freezer space available
- Plenty of storage space

### Business Model
- Social enterprise (donate all profits)
- Premium pricing justified by mission + heritage/artisan quality
- All in-house production to start
- Community partnerships as they develop naturally

## Year-Round Production Calendar

### SPRING (March - May)

#### March: Prep & Planning
**Activities:**
- [ ] Order jars, lids, labels in bulk (400-500 units)
- [ ] Apply for summer farmers markets
- [ ] Recruit volunteers for picking parties
- [ ] Set up social media accounts
- [ ] Test recipes, finalize product line
- [ ] Get licenses/permits (Cottage Food License, Temp Restaurant License)
- [ ] Design labels with mission story

**Production:** None - soil prep, planting

**Time Commitment:** 5-10 hrs/week (planning)

---

#### April: Early Greens
**Harvest:**
- Sorrel (begins)
- Parsley (begins)
- Early herbs

**Production:**
- Sorrel pesto (4 oz jars) - freeze for later sales

**Time Commitment:** Light - 1-2 sessions, 5-10 hrs/week

**Harvest:**

**Time Commitment:** Moderate - 2-3 sessions, 10-15 hrs/week

## Equipment Usage Optimization

### Dehydrator Schedule & Strategy

**June-July: Herbs**
- Dry excess basil, parsley for winter use
- Make herb salts
- Dry edible flowers (if you have them)

**August: Tomatoes (Optional)**
- Tomato leather/fruit leather
- Use up excess tomatoes

**September-November: APPLES (Peak Usage)**
- Run continuously during apple season
- Apple chips from good apples
- Apple peel chips from processing scraps
- Keep batches small for freshness

**Year-Round:**
- Peel scraps from any processing
- Herb preservation
- Gift-quality dried goods

**Dehydrator Tips:**
- Label batches with date
- Store in airtight containers
- Shelf life: 6-12 months
- Energy cost: ~$0.50/batch (24 hrs)

---

### Freezer Strategy & Inventory

**May-August: Freeze Pesto**
- Make large batches when basil is abundant
- Freeze in 4 oz portions
- Store flat in freezer bags (saves space)
- Thaw and jar as "fresh-made" through winter
- Shelf life frozen: 6-8 months

**June-August: Emergency Berry Storage**
- If overwhelmed, freeze berries whole
- Make jam in fall/winter when time allows
- Shelf life frozen: 12 months

**September-October: Apple Sauce**
- If you run out of jars mid-season
- Freeze in quart containers
- Can properly when jars available
- Shelf life frozen: 12 months

**Organization:**
- Label everything with date and contents
- Rotate stock (first in, first out)
- Keep inventory list on freezer door
- Reserve space for emergency overflow

### Canning Equipment & Setup

**Essential Equipment:**
- Large stockpot or water bath canner
- Jar lifter, funnel, bubble remover
- Kitchen scale (for recipes)
- Large cutting boards
- Sharp knives
- Food mill or food processor
- Slow cooker (for apple butter)

**Batch Processing Setup:**
- Clear large workspace
- Production line: wash ‚Üí prep ‚Üí cook ‚Üí fill ‚Üí process
- Label station separate from hot zone
- Cooling rack space for processed jars
- Good ventilation (windows open, fan)

**Peak Season Organization:**
- "Pickle Day" setup: brine pots, jar sterilizing
- "Tomato Day" setup: blanching pot, food mill
- "Apple Day" setup: peeling station, cooking pots, dehydrator
- Keep frequently used items accessible

**Safety Notes:**
- Water bath for high-acid foods (tomatoes, pickles, jams, apples)
- Pressure canner NOT needed for your product line
- Follow tested recipes (don't improvise ratios)
- Check seals 24 hours after processing
- Store in cool, dark place

## Production Tips & Best Practices

### Efficiency Strategies

**Theme Days Save Time:**
- Make one type of brine ‚Üí multiple pickle products
- Process tomatoes once ‚Üí multiple sauces
- Similar techniques across products = muscle memory

**Batch Sizing:**
- Small batches (10-20 jars): Better for learning, variety
- Large batches (50-75 jars): More efficient for staples
- Match batch size to your energy level

**Recipe Scaling:**
- Use spreadsheet to scale ingredients
- Keep notes on what works
- Consistency = repeat customers

**Prep Ahead:**
- Sterilize jars the night before
- Prep ingredients in batches
- Make brines/syrups in advance

---

### Quality Control

**Before Processing:**
- Use ripe but firm produce
- Discard damaged items
- Consistent sizing for even cooking

**During Processing:**
- Accurate measurements (especially acid in pickles)
- Proper headspace in jars
- Remove air bubbles
- Clean jar rims

**After Processing:**
- Check seals (lid should not flex)
- Label immediately (easy to forget!)
- Note batch number/date
- Store properly

**Testing:**
- Taste test before scaling up
- Ask friends/family for feedback
- Adjust recipes if needed
- Document changes

---

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Don't:**
- ‚ùå Over-pack jars (they might not seal)
- ‚ùå Under-process (food safety risk)
- ‚ùå Skip testing new recipes
- ‚ùå Process when exhausted (mistakes happen)
- ‚ùå Forget to label jars (you WILL forget what's what)
- ‚ùå Store in warm/bright areas (degrades quality)
- ‚ùå Try to do everything in year one

**Do:**
- ‚úÖ Start small and build
- ‚úÖ Ask for help during peak times
- ‚úÖ Take breaks (it's marathon, not sprint)
- ‚úÖ Document everything
- ‚úÖ Rest in winter
- ‚úÖ Celebrate successes
